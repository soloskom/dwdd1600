Digital Citizenship

What is Digital Citizenship?

At its core, digital citizenship is the ability to engage in online communities in a way that is responsible, respectful, and safe. It's essentially "netiquette" evolved for the 21st century—a framework for how we should behave and interact with others in the vast digital landscape.Being a good digital citizen isn't just about following rules; it's about understanding the impact of your digital footprint and using technology to contribute positively to the world.

What is Digital Law?

Digital law refers to the legal rights and restrictions that govern technology use within society. Just as physical laws exist to maintain order and protect citizens in the real world, digital laws provide a framework for ethical and legal behavior in the virtual world.

What is AUP?

An AUP, or Acceptable Use Policy, is a set of rules applied by an owner, creator, or administrator of a network, website, or service. It restricts the ways in which the network or system may be used and sets guidelines as to how it should be utilized.

What is Plagarism?

Plagiarism is the act of presenting someone else's work, ideas, or expressions as your own. In the world of digital citizenship, it is considered a major breach of digital ethics and digital law.

Think of it as "intellectual theft." Whether it's intentional or accidental, using someone's words or creative output without giving them proper credit undermines the integrity of the work and the original creator.

What is Academic Dishonesty?

Academic dishonesty is a broad term that covers any type of cheating, fraud, or deceit in an academic setting. While plagiarism is the most famous example, academic dishonesty encompasses a much wider range of behaviors that give a student an unfair advantage or misrepresent their actual knowledge and effort.

In the context of digital citizenship, academic dishonesty often involves the misuse of technology—such as using AI to write essays without permission or using unauthorized websites during an online exam.

What are the rules for AI Generated Content?

In 2026, the rules for AI in the classroom have shifted from outright bans to a framework of "Responsible Integration." While every school has its own Acceptable Use Policy (AUP), most follow a set of core principles designed to balance innovation with academic integrity. This might include using artificial intelligence solely when explicitly given permission, or being prohibited from using it altogether.
