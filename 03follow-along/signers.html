Who Were the Signers

Famous Signers

While 56 men signed the Declaration of Independence in 1776, a handful of them stand out as the most famous, largely due to their massive impact on the founding of the United States and their later roles in history.

John Hancock
The President of the Continental Congress and the first to sign the document. He famously wrote his name so large and boldly that "John Hancock" became a lasting synonym for a signature.
Thomas Jefferson
The principal author of the Declaration of Independence who penned its famous words on liberty and equality. He later served as the first U.S. Secretary of State, second Vice President, and third U.S. President.
Benjamin Franklin
The oldest delegate to sign the document at age 70. An internationally renowned scientist, inventor, and diplomat, his wit and political wisdom were crucial in uniting the colonies.
John Adams
A passionate driving force for independence on the congressional floor who served on the drafting committee. He later became the first Vice President and the second President of the United States.
Samuel Adams
Second cousin to John Adams and a fiery political agitator. As a leader of the Sons of Liberty, he was instrumental in orchestrating early colonial resistance to British taxation.
Benjamin Rush
A prominent physician and social reformer who later helped found Dickinson College and passionately advocated for public education and the abolition of slavery.
Richard Henry Lee
The Virginia delegate who officially introduced the resolution to Congress declaring that the colonies "are, and of right ought to be, free and independent States."
Robert Morris
Known as the "Financier of the Revolution," he used his immense personal wealth and credit to secure the critical loans and supplies necessary to fund the Continental Army.

Additionally, the second cousin of John Adams, Samuel was a fiery political agitator and a leader of the Sons of Liberty in Boston. He was instrumental in orchestrating early resistance to British taxation, including the Boston Tea Party.
